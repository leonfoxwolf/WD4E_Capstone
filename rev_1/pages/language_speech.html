In "First Japanese Lessons" the transcription follows the Hepburn system, so each syllable is written not in Russian, but in English. The system itself is given in the table on the left. This does not affect language learning in any way.

The phonetics of the Japanese language is not very complicated, although some of the sounds are unusual to our ears. So, the Japanese syllable, denoted in Russian transcription as "si", in fact is pronounced as a cross between the Russian "si" and "shchi". In accordance with the accepted transcription, the syllable "ti" is also written, pronounced as something in between the Russian "ti" and "chi". In the formation of the sound "f" lips, unlike our "f", do not participate - it is, rather, a light exhalation. The Japanese sound "in" is similar to the English sound denoted as "w", and "n" if it is at the end of a word is a cross between the English "n" and "ng". Sounds designated in the Latin alphabet as "l", "q", "v" and "x" are not used. The length of the vowel sounds is very important. Usually, when transcribing in writing, it is indicated by a colon after a repetitive sound (for example, "ot: -san" - the second sound "o" sounds longer). The longitude of a sound is a meaningful moment, so it cannot be ignored (judge for yourself: "soojo" is a young girl, "soojo" is an orangutan).

In addition, in Japanese there is no concept of force stress (the stress here is musical, based on the difference in pitch), therefore, there is no concept of "vowel sound in an unstressed position." It is also necessary to note the reduction (loss) of the vowels "y" and "and" in the position between voiceless consonants and "y" in the final syllable "su". As a result, say desu and sita will be pronounced des and sita. In Japanese grammar, the predicate always appears at the end of a sentence, and the definition always comes before the word being defined.

I don't see much point in talking about all the techniques, because it's easier to buy a book and start learning from it. However, below I decided to make a small nano-dictionary.

  • Hai - Yes
  • Ohayo:(gozaimas') - Good morning
  • Konnichi wa - Good afternoon
  • Komban wa - Good evening
  • Sayonara - Goodbye
  • Arigato:(gozaimas') - Thank you
  • Do:itash'mash'te - Please (not at all)
  • Iie - No
  • Ii des' ka? - It's good?
  • Dame des' - This is bad
  • Chigaimas' - This is not true
  • Gomen nasai - I'm very sorry
  • Wasuremash'ta - I forgot
  • Machigaimash'ta - I was wrong
